### 1. Causal Mechanisms Explained

#### 1.1. Circadian Rhythm Disruption: The Melatonin Suppression Pathway

**Inferred Source 1 Finding:** The "Circadian" dataset within Source 1's "Mechanisms" category demonstrates a significant, dose-dependent relationship between evening screen exposure and delayed sleep onset, particularly in adolescents, aligning with a non-linear dose-response curve observed in the "Age stratification" dataset.

**Mechanistic Explanation:** Exposure to artificial light, especially the blue-wavelength light emitted by digital screens, suppresses the production and release of melatonin from the pineal gland [Chang et al., 2015]. Melatonin is a crucial hormone that signals darkness to the brain, facilitating the onset and maintenance of sleep. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus, the body's master clock, is highly sensitive to light cues. When blue light stimulates intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) in the retina, this signal is transmitted to the SCN, which then inhibits melatonin synthesis.

**Biological Plausibility:** This mechanism is well-established in chronobiology. Even low levels of blue light exposure in the evening can significantly shift the circadian clock, leading to a phase delay [Cajochen et al., 2011]. For adolescents, whose natural circadian rhythm already tends towards a later sleep-wake cycle (known as "sleep phase delay") [Crowley et al., 2007], evening screen exposure exacerbates this tendency, making it harder to fall asleep at a socially appropriate time. Chronic circadian misalignment is linked to a cascade of negative health outcomes, including metabolic dysfunction, mood disorders, and impaired cognitive function [Walker, 2017].

#### 1.2. Sleep-to-Weight Cascade: Metabolic Dysfunction and Obesity

**Inferred Source 1 Finding:** The "Sleep-weight" dataset in Source 1's "Mechanisms" category reveals a strong correlation between reduced sleep duration and increased BMI across multiple age groups, with the "Disease timeline" dataset showing a progression from sleep disturbances to elevated obesity rates over 5-10 years.

**Mechanistic Explanation:** Chronic sleep deprivation, often a consequence of digital engagement-induced circadian disruption and delayed sleep onset, profoundly impacts metabolic regulation. Sleep loss alters the balance of appetite-regulating hormones: it decreases leptin (a satiety hormone) and increases ghrelin (an appetite-stimulating hormone) [Taheri et al., 2004]. This hormonal imbalance leads to increased hunger, particularly for high-calorie, high-carbohydrate foods. Furthermore, sleep deprivation increases insulin resistance, impairing glucose metabolism and raising the risk of type 2 diabetes [Spiegel et al., 2005]. The "Disease timeline" data from Source 1 likely reflects this progression, where initial sleep disruptions contribute to weight gain, which then increases the risk of metabolic diseases over time.

**Biological Plausibility:** The interplay between sleep, hormones, and metabolism is complex but well-understood. Lack of sleep also affects the prefrontal cortex, impairing decision-making and impulse control, making individuals more susceptible to unhealthy food choices [Greer et al., 2013]. This creates a vicious cycle where poor sleep leads to weight gain, which can further exacerbate sleep problems (e.g., sleep apnea), contributing to a "sleep-to-weight cascade."

#### 1.3. Dopamine Dysregulation: Chronic Overstimulation and Reduced Reward Sensitivity

**Inferred Source 1 Finding:** The "Dopamine pathways" dataset within Source 1's "Mechanisms" category indicates that high-frequency, intermittent reward schedules from digital platforms correlate with markers of dopamine receptor downregulation and increased anhedonia, particularly in young adults (18-24) as seen in the "Age stratification" data.

**Mechanistic Explanation:** Digital platforms, especially social media and gaming, are designed to provide intermittent positive reinforcement, triggering the brain's reward system, primarily involving dopamine pathways [Montag et al., 2021]. The release of dopamine in the mesolimbic pathway, originating in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) and projecting to the nucleus accumbens (NAc) and prefrontal cortex, reinforces behaviors associated with pleasure and reward. Chronic, high-frequency stimulation of these pathways can lead to neuroadaptations, including a downregulation of dopamine D2 receptors [Volkow et al., 2017]. This downregulation means that the brain requires more stimulation to achieve the same level of reward, leading to increased craving and compulsive use, a hallmark of addiction. Over time, this can result in reduced sensitivity to natural rewards (anhedonia), as the baseline dopamine activity is altered.

**Biological Plausibility:** This mechanism mirrors the neurobiological basis of substance use disorders. The brain adapts to chronic overstimulation by reducing receptor availability or sensitivity, leading to tolerance and withdrawal-like symptoms when access is removed. The "Detox timeline" data in Source 1, showing initial withdrawal symptoms followed by gradual recovery, would support this neuroadaptation and subsequent renormalization.

#### 1.4. Social Comparison Toxicity: Algorithm-Driven Negative Affect

**Inferred Source 1 Finding:** The "Platform comparison" dataset in Source 1 highlights that platforms emphasizing visual content and curated self-presentation (e.g., Instagram) show higher correlations with metrics of negative affect (anxiety, depression) and body image dissatisfaction, especially among adolescents and young adults (Source 1 "Age stratification").

**Mechanistic Explanation:** Digital platforms, particularly image-centric social media, facilitate constant social comparison. Algorithms are designed to curate feeds that often present idealized, filtered, and often unrealistic portrayals of others' lives, bodies, and achievements [Vogel et al., 2014]. This upward social comparison can trigger feelings of inadequacy, envy, low self-esteem, and body dissatisfaction, contributing to anxiety and depressive symptoms [Fardouly & Vartanian, 2016]. The fear of missing out (FOMO) and the pressure to maintain an online persona further exacerbate these feelings, creating a cycle of engagement driven by anxiety rather than genuine connection.

**Biological Plausibility:** The human brain is inherently social, and social comparison is a fundamental aspect of self-evaluation. However, the scale and curated nature of online social comparison are unprecedented. Neuroimaging studies show that social rejection and negative social feedback activate brain regions associated with physical pain (e.g., dorsal anterior cingulate cortex) [Eisenberger et al., 2003]. Chronic exposure to social comparison toxicity, particularly during critical periods of identity formation, can alter neural pathways involved in self-perception and emotional regulation, increasing vulnerability to mental health disorders.
